## Opening scene: “The Troll’s Back”

### Read-aloud / opening description

The deed in your possession feels much smaller than the building before you.

Trollskull Manor rises above the alley like a great carcass of old wealth: four stories, narrow balconies, a jutting turret, and crooked chimneys blackened by years of rain and neglect. The shutters hang unevenly. One upper window stands cracked like a missing tooth. The sign above the entry sways in the wind with a faint, tired creak.

When you force open the front door, the smell hits first: dust, old wood, stale beer, mildew, and vinegar gone sharp in split casks. The taproom beyond is a wreck of broken chairs, warped tabletops, tarnished forks and cups, and drifts of grime thick enough to remember footprints.

Then, from somewhere deeper in the room, a child’s voice cries out:

“Run! The troll’s got in!”

A wooden arrow skips across the floor and clatters off a table leg.

A moment later, three children explode out from behind the bar and overturned furniture as if springing an ambush.

One is a lanky girl of about ten with a wooden sword and the hard, measuring stare of someone who expects the world to cheat first. Another is a rounder boy in a cloak, clutching a toy wand in one hand and a battered stuffed owlbear in the other. The third is a skinny tiefling boy with an eyepatch, a little bow, and a grin that suggests he thinks this is the best thing that has happened all tenday.

The lanky girl flashes a quick series of signs at the others.

The tiefling boy points dramatically at you and shouts, “I told you! I told you the troll skull was real!”

Before anyone can answer, somewhere upstairs a glass bottle slides off a shelf and shatters on its own.

The children freeze.

The cloaked boy whispers, “...it doesn’t usually do that that loud.”

## What is actually happening in the room

This scene works best if you think of it as three overlapping realities:

1. **The kids are playing.**
   They were in the middle of a mock battle against the “troll skull ghost.”

2. **The manor is genuinely haunted.**
   Lif is reacting to strangers entering, but not yet in a lethal or even aggressive way—just enough to make the house feel alive. The published chapter makes him the former barkeep whose poltergeist antics escalate through smashed objects and written warnings. 

3. **The party is being sized up.**
   The urchins are deciding whether these new owners are marks, monsters, or maybe useful adults.

That gives you comedy, eeriness, and social texture all at once.

## The urchins in the scene

### Nat

Nat is the axis of the scene. In the remix she is deaf, the leader, and uses a sign language she invented and taught the others. 

She does not recoil first. She studies first.

**Initial posture:** one foot forward, toy sword raised, chin tilted up like she’s defending her fort.

**How to play her:**

* suspicious, not timid
* annoyed at being interrupted

* signs quickly to the boys, then waits to see if any of the adults know signs

**First beats for Nat:**

A good reveal line through Jenks or Squiddly:

> “She says you walk like you own the place.”
> Beat.
> “Which, I mean... maybe you do?”

**Useful Jenks lines:**

**How to play him:**

**Useful Squiddly lines:**

He should say one thing that is outrageous enough to get a laugh and one thing useful enough to earn his keep.

You want the urchins to become:

* mascots

So give them a reason to stay and chatter.

## Suggested scene flow at the table

### Beat 1: The manor entrance

Give them the smell, disrepair, and scale. Let them feel the place before anyone talks. This matches the published description of the abandoned taproom and reinforces that Trollskull is a project, not a free reward. 

### Beat 2: Toy ambush

The arrow clatters. The children burst out. Fast, funny, messy.

### Beat 3: Recognition and sorting

The kids realize these are adults, maybe owners, maybe not. The party realizes the “intruders” are three children armed with toys and nerve.

### Beat 4: The ghost lands a punchline

Bottle breaks upstairs or writing appears in dust.

### Beat 5: Legend and rumor

The urchins explain the troll-skull story, badly and with interruptions.

### Beat 6: Test of character

The party’s response determines tone:

* kindness -> the urchins become attached immediately
* threats -> the urchins flee, but keep watching from outside
* curiosity -> they turn into excitable guides
* mockery -> Nat hardens, Jenks wilts, Squiddly mouths off

The kids offer to help, explore, or “keep watch.” The remix specifically says that if friendly, they’ll help sweep up and holler on street corners to attract customers. 

That gives you your closing line.

# Scene: “A Neighbor Calls”

### Setup

The party is in the taproom. Dust everywhere. Broken chairs. Maybe the urchins are still around, maybe half-hidden on the stairs or peeking through the doorway. Someone is discussing repairs, budgets, or whether the place is salvageable.

Then there is a measured knock at the open front doorframe.

Not a friendly knock.
Not a nervous knock.
A knock from someone who already assumes he belongs in the conversation.

---

## Read-aloud

A man stands in the doorway with the posture of someone trying very hard to look prosperous.

He is broad-shouldered, weathered by northern winds, and dressed in clothes that are expensive at a glance and disappointing on closer inspection: a good coat with a frayed cuff, polished boots with one sole wearing thin, a handsome signet worked in cheap metal, and a walking cane chosen more for display than use. He smells faintly of clove, wet wool, and fresh pitch.

His eyes travel across the room in a single quick sweep: the broken furniture, the warped floorboards, the stained bar, the cracked windows, the casks gone sour.

He smiles.

“Ah,” he says, with the tone of a man offering congratulations at a funeral. “So it’s true. Volo really did hand the place over.”

He removes one glove finger by finger.

“Emmet Frewn. Frewn’s Brews.” He inclines his head toward the alley outside. “Your nearest competitor. And, naturally, your neighbor.”

His gaze drifts up toward the ceiling beams.

“Well. Neighbor, at any rate.”

---

## The visual read on Frewn

Play him as a man who is trying to present as established, but is visibly overextended.

Good visual cues:

* a dab of roof tar on one cuff or thumb
* a little plaster dust on his sleeve
* a signet ring that looks impressive until closely examined
* polished boots that are starting to split at the seams
* a bottle of middling spirits as a “gift,” wrapped too neatly to be spontaneous

That reinforces the published material: he spends on big, showy things and cuts corners on the practical details. He is also already in trouble with guilds because he tried to repair his roof himself and offended a plumber. 

---

## His attitude

Frewn is not here to threaten them. He is here to:

* inspect the competition
* measure their competence
* find out how badly they can be hurt
* establish a surface-level civility he can later hide behind

He should be **pleasant, faintly condescending, and too interested in specifics**.

He is the kind of man who says “as a courtesy” when he means “for leverage.”

---

## Dialogue spine

### Opening

> “I thought it only proper to introduce myself. North Ward custom. We are not the Dock Ward, after all.”

### Looking around the ruin

> “It has bones, I’ll grant it. Fine frontage. Good alley traffic. Shame about the rest.”

### On having wanted the place

> “I made a serious offer for Trollskull Manor. Very serious. But Volo, as is his way, preferred romance to arithmetic.”

### On their chances

> “Do forgive me, but you do know what this will cost to reopen properly, yes?”

### On guilds

> “The guilds will smile while they tally your ruin. Roofers, cellarers, coopers, brewers, butchers, bakers—every last one with a hand out and a rulebook in the other.”

That tracks with Trollskull’s setup pressure from the book and also lets him telegraph his own anti-guild grudge.  

### On “neighborly help”

> “Still, no reason we cannot be civil. There’s room in this alley for… different sorts of establishments.”

### The knife under the velvet

> “North Ward patrons can be loyal. They can also be unforgiving. One bad cask, one kitchen mishap, one whisper about vermin, and a house can spend a season recovering.”

That line quietly foreshadows his future rat-infestation rumor campaign and wererat sabotage without announcing it. 

---

## What he is actually fishing for

During the conversation, Frewn should casually try to learn:

* when they plan to reopen
* whether they have coin for repairs
* whether they mean to run it themselves or hire staff
* whether they have cellar access and plumbing problems
* whether they’ve already engaged the guilds
* whether they are naïve enough to accept a “cheap” recommendation

He should ask these like a businessman making conversation, not a spy taking notes.

Examples:

> “Are you refurbishing the cellar as well, or only the common room?”

> “Hiring staff, I assume? Or are you planning to pour every cup yourselves?”

> “Have the roofers seen this yet? Gods preserve you if they haven’t.”

> “You do intend to join the Fellowship, I hope. They can be tiresome when slighted.”

He should not ask more than two or three of these before the players start getting suspicious.

---

## A good scene structure

### Beat 1: The entrance

He knocks and waits just long enough to seem polite.

### Beat 2: The appraisal

He surveys the room like a buyer who has lost an auction and is still calculating whether the winner overpaid.

### Beat 3: The introduction

He names himself and his tavern.

### Beat 4: The needle

He mentions, calmly, that he had wanted this place himself.

### Beat 5: The “advice”

He offers practical talk about costs, guilds, and clientele.

### Beat 6: The test

He watches how the party responds:

* boastful -> he marks them as reckless
* practical -> he marks them as dangerous
* confused -> he sees an opening
* rude -> he gets to feel justified

### Beat 7: The parting gift

He leaves something behind: a bottle, a business card equivalent, or a recommendation that is maybe useful and maybe poisoned.

## My recommended version of the scene

This is the version I would actually run.

### Read-aloud plus action

Frewn steps carefully over the threshold without waiting for a full invitation, then stops just inside as if the room itself offends him.

He lifts a handkerchief to his nose for a moment, glances toward the vinegar casks, and gives a low sympathetic hum.

“Emmet Frewn,” he says. “Frewn’s Brews. Just down the alley.”

His eyes linger on the bar.

“I had hoped this place would be mine, you know. But one cannot compete with a famous man in a sentimental mood.”

He produces a bottle from under one arm and offers it with a smile too thin to be generous.

“A housewarming gift. Neighborly courtesy.”

The bottle is real, but middling. Not poisoned, not trapped, just cheap liquor dressed up in respectable glass.

Then he begins his walk through the room—not nosing through drawers or touching belongings, but inspecting sightlines, floorboards, and ceiling seams with the practiced eye of a tavern owner.

“This beam will need replacing,” he says. “And that window. And you’ll want the cellar seen to before summer damp sets in. Unless you mean to serve mushroom wine and lung rot.”

He gives a brief, humorless chuckle, checking to see who laughs.

Then, more softly:

“Forgive me. Professional habit. I understand these houses.”

He turns back to them.

“That is why I came. You are new to the alley. I am not. If you mean to reopen, you should at least go in with clear eyes.”

> “I wanted the property. I did not get it. That is commerce, not tragedy.”

If they ask whether he wants them gone:

> “I want the best house in the alley to prosper. Naturally, I also want mine to be that house.”

That gives you rivalry without confession.

---

## How to end the scene

End it before he wears out his welcome.

My preferred exit:

From outside, a cart rattles past. Frewn glances toward the alley and pulls his gloves back on.

“Well,” he says, “I won’t keep you from your ruinously expensive dreams.”

He gives a small bow.

“When you choose a name for the place, make it a good one. North Ward patrons forgive poor ale before they forgive poor taste.”

He turns to go, then pauses at the door.

“Oh—and do take care with the cellar. Old buildings collect all sorts.”

He leaves.

That final cellar remark can mean damp, mold, rats, ghosts, structural problems, or sabotage. It is exactly the kind of line a petty rival likes because it can always be defended later.

---

## After he leaves

Do not immediately confirm he is a saboteur. Let the party argue about him.

Give them one of these small stingers:

* the bottle label is ornate but the liquor is mediocre
* he left a faint smear of roof tar on the bar
* one of the urchins says he’s been staring at the manor for days
* Lif makes some subtle disapproving gesture after he leaves, like slamming a cupboard or tipping the bottle over

That last one is especially good. If the ghost dislikes Frewn, the players may trust that instinct before they know why.

---

## What not to do

Do not have him:

* threaten them openly
* insult them directly for more than one line
* mention wererats
* monologue his entire plan
* start a shouting match

That would be wasting the slow burn. His printed scheme escalates from observation and rumor into sabotage and Watch complaints. The first scene should feel like the beginning of that escalation, not the climax. 

If you want, I can turn this into a **boxed scene with DM prompts and branching responses**, or write the **next Frewn scene** where he tries to “help” by recommending a suspiciously cheap hire.


# 3. Guild Visit (Soukev)
Do **not** play Soukaev as another obstacle. Play him as the first person who treats Trollskull Manor like a real business instead of a haunted joke.

That is the key calibration.

The source material already does the structural work for you: Trollskull Manor is meant to be **refurnished, rebuilt, and reopened**, and Chapter 2 assumes the party will deal with guilds to make that happen. The published text says repairs to the walls and roof require the approval and oversight of the **Carpenters’, Roofers’, and Plaisterers’ Guild**, while the remix replaces the abstract one-time expense with a concrete guild visit: **Soukaev** handling **roof and wall repairs for 250 gp**. The remix also explicitly says to roleplay these guild visits, and notes that **not all services are required and others can be negotiated**.   

So the encounter should communicate:

* this is expensive
* this is normal
* this is solvable
* this is the cost of entering Waterdeep’s civic and commercial life

Not: “surprise, your reward is unusable.”

## The tone you want

Soukaev should feel like:

* practical
* unsentimental
* mildly overworked
* faintly annoyed by amateurs
* quietly pleased that someone is finally fixing the place properly

He is not there to scare them off. He is there to tell them, in professional terms, what “properly” means.

And because Frewn already stiffed or defied this guild, Soukaev should also serve as a quiet contrast: **this is what competence looks like**. Frewn cuts corners; Soukaev names the work, prices it, and stands by it. The published text specifically says Frewn got into trouble by trying to fix his roof himself in defiance of this guild. 

## When to place the scene

Best placement: **shortly after Frewn leaves**.

That gives you a clean juxtaposition:

* Frewn says guilds are bloodsuckers
* then an actual guild rep arrives and proves the opposite: expensive, yes; predatory, no

That undercuts Frewn without you having to editorialize.

If the party is already irritated or suspicious after Frewn, Soukaev becomes reassuring simply by being honest.

---

# Scene concept: “The Estimate”

### Entrance

A knock at the frame. Not the hesitant knock of a customer and not the oily social knock Frewn used.

This is a **workman’s knock**: brief, direct, efficient.

Outside stands Soukaev with:

* a rolled waxed parchment tube
* a bit of red chalk or carpenter’s pencil tucked behind one ear
* a folding rule or measuring rod
* boots that have actually seen roofs
* one or two laborers/apprentices trailing behind with clipboards, ladders, or a satchel of tools

He does not look impressed by the manor. He looks like he has already priced three worse buildings before breakfast.

### Opening line

> “Soukaev. Carpenters’, Roofers’, and Plaisterers’ Guild. I’m told this place may be reopening, which means either you need an estimate or I need to stop you from killing someone with falling masonry.”

That line does three things:

* it gets a laugh
* it makes the stakes real
* it frames him as blunt, not hostile

If the party bristles, he shrugs and says:

> “Good. Pride means you may actually intend to finish the job.”

---

# What the encounter should actually do

## 1. He inspects, he doesn’t sell

Frewn sells. Soukaev assesses.

He should immediately start noticing things:

* tapping beams with a knuckle
* sighting along warped wall lines
* looking up through the rafters
* checking plaster cracks
* muttering roof pitch notes
* asking who has been upstairs
* asking whether anyone has already been injured by loose timber or glass

He should be good enough at his job that the party trusts his conclusions even when they dislike the numbers.

Useful observations:

> “Front wall is sound enough. South side needs patching.”

> “The roof wants work now, not after the next hard rain.”

> “Some of this plaster is cosmetic. Some of it is attached to regret.”

> “You can sleep in a leaky house. You cannot safely serve drink in one if pieces of ceiling fall into the cups.”

That last line matters. It shifts repairs from “beautification” to “minimum viable tavern.”

---

## 2. He gives them a structured estimate

This is where you avoid scaring them off.

Do **not** present it as a giant faceless number. Present it as:

* what is essential
* what can wait
* what is included
* what happens if they delay

The remix gives you the source-of-truth number: **250 gp** for Soukaev’s guild to repair the roof and walls.  

So I would have him break that into plain language:

> “To make the place structurally fit for trade: roof patching, damaged wall sections, exterior weatherproofing, interior plaister where failure is likely, and inspection sign-off. Two hundred and fifty dragons.”

Then immediately narrow it:

> “That is not for luxury. That is not for fashion. That is not for making the place pretty. That is for making it legal, serviceable, and less likely to kill a guest.”

That distinction is important. It tells the players the cost is real, but not arbitrary.

Then give them the release valve:

> “Furniture, decoration, clever notions, private rooms, balcony refinishing, carved nonsense—those are separate problems. You need not solve every problem in the same tenday.”

That keeps the dream alive.

---

## 3. He offers phased realism

The remix says some services can be negotiated. Use that. 

I would not reduce the **250 gp** headline, because that undercuts the source structure. But I would let Soukaev discuss **timing and sequencing**:

### Soukaev’s practical framing

> “If your question is ‘must we do all of this before opening,’ the answer is no. If your question is ‘which portion must be done before strangers pay to stand under this roof,’ the answer is most of it.”

Then he can break it into phases:

**Immediate trade-safe work**

* roof and wall stabilization
* the guild’s sign-off for public use

**Can wait until after opening**

* upper-floor refinishing
* decorative plaster
* nicer trim
* cosmetic repairs in unused rooms

This preserves the cost floor while reducing despair.

If you want an even softer approach, Soukaev can say:

> “You do not need a manor to start a tavern. You need a safe common room, a dry roof, and enough sense not to invite inspectors before you’re ready.”

That line makes the project feel achievable.

---

## 4. He should quietly validate the party’s plan

One of the risks here is making the players feel like reopening is a trap. So Soukaev should, at least once, speak as though reopening is the normal and reasonable thing to do.

Example:

> “This alley could use a proper house again.”

Or:

> “Shame to let a building like this rot. It was made to have people in it.”

Or:

> “If you do it properly, the place will stand for years.”

That matters more than friendliness. It gives the party permission to invest.

It also aligns with the published expectation that the characters can personalize and reopen the place, and that repairing it gradually wins Lif over.  

---

# A full version of the scene

### Read-aloud

A sharp rap sounds against the open doorframe.

Outside stands a broad-shouldered human in a weather-darkened coat with a measuring rod in one hand and a scroll case under the other arm. Chalk dust clings to one sleeve. He has the expression of a man who has climbed too many ladders to waste time on charm.

Two younger workers linger behind him with a satchel of tools and a board for notes.

He glances up at the manor’s roofline, then at the cracked plaster around the entry, then finally at you.

“Soukaev,” he says. “Carpenters’, Roofers’, and Plaisterers’ Guild.”

His gaze shifts past you into the gloom of the taproom.

“If you’re the new owners, I’ve come to see whether this building wants repairs or last rites.”

---

### His inspection

Without swagger, without apology, he steps inside and starts looking.

He taps a support post with his knuckles.
Looks up along the ceiling seam.
Scrapes one thumb over a cracked wall and studies the dust.
Squints toward the stairs.
Glances to any stained patch beneath the roofline.

Then:

> “How long since rain came through the upper floor?”

If they do not know:

> “Mm. Long enough for hidden damage, then.”

He crouches near a bad patch of wall:

> “Surface failure here. More troubling there.”

Points upward.

> “That roof has held through neglect, which is a credit to the original work. It will not continue holding out of sentiment.”

That is a good guildsman line: professional respect for earlier craftsmen.

---

### The estimate

After a minute or two, he unrolls a parchment and speaks like a man dictating facts:

> “For roof patching, wall repair, necessary plaister replacement, weather sealing, labor, materials, and guild sign-off: two hundred and fifty dragons.”

Let the number land.

Then he adds, before the players can spiral:

> “That is the cost to make the place fit for business. Not elegant. Not fashionable. Fit.”

Then, if needed:

> “You are not being asked to restore a noble villa. You are being asked not to poison guests with damp rot and falling lime.”

That line keeps the stakes practical.

---

### The reassurance

Then give them the pivot.

> “Listen carefully. This place does not need to be perfect. It needs to be sound.”

He starts ticking points off on his fingers.

> “Common room safe. Roof dry enough. Street-facing walls respectable. No loose plaister over patrons’ heads. The rest can be done as coin allows.”

That is the heart of the scene.

You are telling the party: yes, the cost is real, but the project is finite.

# Soukaev’s personality

I would make him dry rather than warm.

## Mannerisms

* rubs plaster dust between thumb and forefinger
* speaks in short, declarative sentences
* answers nonsense with silence
* respects direct questions
* visibly dislikes anyone pretending to know construction who doesn’t

## What he likes

* practicality
* honest budgeting
* people who ask good questions
* anyone who clearly intends to do proper work

## What annoys him

* haggling based on vibes
* boasting
* “my friend can do it cheaper”
* comparison to Frewn
* adventurers who think carpentry is just hitting wood until it obeys

# Good dialogue for him

### On cost

> “Expensive, yes. More expensive after a collapse.”

### On the building

> “Old does not mean unsound. Neglected does not mean cheap.”

### On reopening

> “You’ve got a good shell here. Better than some houses still serving ale.”

### On Frewn, if pressed

> “I do not discuss other clients. Nor men who decline to become clients.”

That is the best version. It signals history without monologuing about Frewn.

### On guilds

> “You are not paying only for labor. You are paying for the city not asking who approved this work.”

That is pure Waterdeep.

### On phased repairs

> “Open the ground floor first. Earn coin. Improve the rest after. Sensible people do this every year.”

That line is probably the single most useful one for your goal.

# How to keep the players from feeling trapped

This is where DMs often overplay the guild scene.

Do **not** have Soukaev imply:

* they need every service immediately
* they are fools for trying
* the guilds are eager to crush them
* the manor is a financial death spiral

The remix does not support that tone. It supports a sequence of entertaining guild interactions and negotiated services while the PCs build investment in Trollskull.  

So Soukaev should be expensive, but stabilizing.

He is the first NPC to put the project into manageable categories.

---

# Best possible button at the end of the scene

After the estimate, after a few questions, Soukaev rolls up the parchment and says:

> “If you mean to do it, do it before the next hard rain. If you do not, board the upper windows and stop pretending the place is about to be a tavern.”

Then, after a beat:

> “But if you do mean to do it—properly—I’ll put your name on the schedule.”

That final line is crucial. It is a door opening, not closing.

# Optional Lif beat

Use Lif to endorse Soukaev.

After Soukaev gives the estimate, have something subtle happen:

* a warped shutter stops banging
* a chair slides out beside the bar
* dust stirs off a beam Soukaev just identified
* an unseen presence nudges a broken bit of plaster loose exactly where he pointed

That reinforces two things from the source:

* the manor really does need work
* repairing and renovating the tavern is the path to winning Lif over

It also makes the expense feel narratively justified instead of purely mechanical.

You can let them negotiate:

* payment timing
* maybe small nonessential add-ons

But if the 250 gp repair number evaporates immediately, Trollskull Manor stops feeling like an investment and starts feeling like set dressing. The remix specifically uses these guild interactions to create personal investment in the tavern.

# 4. Threestrings and Vajra Safahr
Make these two beats feel **deliberately different**.

If Frewn and Soukaev are both in-person civic-commercial scenes, then Threestrings should feel like the city quietly opening a side door, while Vajra’s sending should feel like the city’s power structure putting a hand on the party’s shoulder. That contrast is already supported by the source material: Threestrings in the remix is a **Harper spy using busking as cover**, while Vajra is the Blackstaff, runs the Gray Hands, and canonically prefers to contact operatives by **sending**. The official Harper hook is also more oblique and social: a message that says **Renaer recommended them**, with **opera tickets** and instructions to meet **Mirt at intermission**.    

So I would run them like this:

## Threestrings’ visit: “A Favor for a Song”

This should happen **after** the urchins and Frewn, ideally once the place has gone a little quieter. The party has had their excitement, their local rivalry, and their first hard reality check. Now give them a visitor who makes Trollskull feel like a place where stories start.

### Tone

Warm, sly, personal, and just a little theatrical.

He should not walk in like a recruiter. He should walk in like a familiar nuisance who has decided to become useful.

### What he is doing, structurally

You are using the remix’s version of Threestrings as your foundation: he is a goofy, seemingly harmless bard, but in truth he is a Harper operative who gathers intelligence under cover of performance. That makes him a much better first Harper touchpoint than a random paper bird out of nowhere. At the same time, the official Harper recruitment mechanism is good and worth preserving: the faction’s overt invitation can still be a **Renaer-backed opera invitation to meet Mirt**.  

So let Threestrings be the **human bridge** to the official hook.

---

### Read-aloud

The alley outside has begun to dim toward evening by the time a familiar, badly strummed chord drifts in through the open door.

A moment later Mattrim “Threestrings” Mereg leans into the doorway as if he has always belonged there. His purple-and-green clothes are a little too bright for the dusty ruin around him, and his scarred lute looks one strong sneeze away from collapse.

He plucks another chord — thin, crooked, but somehow still charming.

“Well,” he says, peering around the wrecked taproom with a lopsided smile, “this is either the beginning of a glorious enterprise or the last sad verse of a cautionary ballad.”

His eyes flick to the broken furniture, the dust, the warped bar, and then back to the party.

“I came to find out which.”

---

### How to roleplay him

He should be visibly the same man they know from the Yawning Portal: loose, funny, musically self-deprecating. But now he is paying attention in a different way. He notices:

* who is actually working
* who is pretending to work
* who watches the door
* who protects the children
* who listens before speaking

That matters because the Harpers use people as instruments to get at the truth, prefer discreet contacts in taverns, and are already suspicious of the city’s rising violence.

### Opening conversation

He should start with mundane talk and let the players invite depth.

Good early lines:

> “You’ve got good bones here. Rotten boards in places, but good bones.”

> “Frewn been by yet? No, don’t answer. I can smell injured pride from the street.”

> “Funny thing about a tavern — folk tell the truth in them by mistake.”

That last line is your Harper thesis statement.

If the party offers him a drink, a seat, or asks him to play, he becomes more candid. If they’re cold, he stays light and leaves the hook behind anyway.

---

### The pivot

After a few minutes, he stops playing and grows just a shade more serious.

> “Renaer speaks well of you. That’s not a recommendation he gives away freely.”

He reaches into his coat and produces a folded note — or, better, a tiny folded **paper bird** which flutters to the table or bar before settling still.

Then:

> “There are people in this city who prefer to ask questions in places where no one notices questions being asked. A busy tavern. A theater box. A funeral. The trick is making it all seem like ordinary life.”

That fits the official Harper profile very well: they operate behind the scenes, use inns and taverns for business, and count Renaer and Mirt among their key Waterdeep figures.

He nudges the note toward them.

Inside, it reads:

> **Renaer tells us you are a good bet. He bought you tickets to the opera tonight at the Lightsinger Theater in the Sea Ward. If you are interested, meet Mirt at intermission. Private Box C. Formal attire is required for admittance.** 

---

### Why this works better than a straight reveal

Do **not** have Threestrings say, “Hello, I am a Harper spy.” That is flatter, less useful, and honestly less consistent with the faction.

Instead, let him be the man who says:

> “Call it an introduction. Call it a test. Call it the city deciding whether you’re worth another look.”

That keeps the romance of the Harpers intact.

### What he is really evaluating

He wants to know:

* whether the party can keep a confidence
* whether they understand subtlety
* whether they think in terms of the city, not just themselves
* whether they are the sort who can hear a whisper in a noisy room

That is very Harper.

### If they ask directly, “Are you a Harper?”

He smiles, plucks two notes, and says:

> “I’m a bard with an unfortunate instrument and better company than I deserve.”

If pressed harder:

> “I know people who prefer tyrants frustrated, secrets exposed, and innocent folk left standing. If that offends you, then no, I know no such people at all.”

That is enough.

---

### Best ending beat

As he turns to go, he pauses and looks back over the ruined common room.

> “Fix this place. Properly, I mean. Waterdeep doesn’t just need another tavern. It needs rooms where the right people can meet at the right time and nobody thinks twice about it.”

Then he taps the opera note.

> “And if you come tonight, wear something that suggests you belong where rich people lie to each other politely.”

Then he leaves, humming a tune just a little better than he ought to be able to play on three strings.

## Vajra’s sending: “Bring Your Friends.”

This should be much shorter and sharper.

Do not turn the sending into a full conversation scene. That would blunt its effect. The official text is clear: Vajra hears of Renaer’s rescue, sends a short direct summons to **one** character, and if refused, tries a **different** party member the next day. The message itself is simple and commanding: **“I am Vajra Safahr, the Blackstaff. Come to Blackstaff Tower in the Castle Ward at once. Bring your friends.”** The same section also notes that sending is her preferred way to communicate with operatives.

### My recommendation on recipient

Send it first to **Jonathan**.

That is the most stable choice unless you specifically want to unsettle the table. He is a paladin, noble-adjacent, justice-oriented, and likely to take the civic weight of the summons seriously. If you want a more uncanny result, send it to **Davy** instead, but that makes the beat more about surprise than about calling the party upward into the city’s machinery.

### Tone

Sudden, intimate, and impossible to ignore.

This is not charm. This is jurisdiction.

---

### Read-aloud

The work has settled into an ordinary rhythm — dust, broken boards, muttered plans, maybe one of the urchins talking too loudly — when one of you suddenly hears a woman’s voice, clear as a bell struck inside your own skull.

Not from the doorway.
Not from the stairs.
Not from anywhere in the room.

From nowhere and everywhere at once.

Calm. Controlled. Impatient only because she is accustomed to being obeyed.

> “I am Vajra Safahr, the Blackstaff. Come to Blackstaff Tower in the Castle Ward at once. Bring your friends.”

And then the voice is gone.

The room is exactly as it was a heartbeat ago — except for the fact that your pulse is not.

---

### How to play the recipient’s reaction

Give the targeted player a second before anyone else knows.

Then ask:

> “Do you say that aloud?”

That gives them ownership of the reveal.

If they repeat it to the group, let the room react:

* one urchin says, “Who?”
* another says, “The Blackstaff?”
* and Nat, if present, goes very still because even street kids know that title has weight

### What this beat communicates

This is where the Harpers and Gray Hands should diverge hard.

Threestrings says: *You interest the city’s shadows.*
Vajra says: *You interest the city’s defenses.*

That is consistent with the faction write-ups: Gray Hands are for people willing to swear to defend Waterdeep, and Force Grey is Waterdeep’s elite crisis-response tool when ordinary authorities are insufficient. Vajra personally selects and directs these people.

---

### If they hesitate

You do not need Vajra to answer questions here. Sending allows reply, but I would use that sparingly.

If the recipient sends back something like, “Why?” or “Who are you really?”, her reply should be brief:

> “Because I do not summon idlers.”
> or
> “Because Waterdeep has noticed you.”
> or
> “Because Renaer’s judgment carries weight with me.”

That last line is grounded in the book’s premise that she is Renaer’s friend and heard quickly of his rescue. 

If they say no, honor the text: she does not argue; she simply tries a different party member a day later.

---

### Small embellishment from the remix

The remix gives you a little more emotional context for Vajra than the base chapter does: she was rescued years ago by Renaer and his circle and now commands Force Grey, with close long-term ties to figures like Laraelra and Meloon. That means she does not need to be played as coldly bureaucratic. She can be severe, but not impersonal. She is a woman with history, power, and reasons to trust Renaer’s instincts even when she worries he is too trusting.

So even in a short sending, there can be the slightest undertone of:
*Renaer vouched for you. Don’t make me regret listening to him.*

---

## Best sequencing between the two

I would not stack them back-to-back with no air.

Best order:

1. Threestrings in person, at dusk or evening
2. Let the party talk
3. Vajra’s sending later that night, or the next morning during work

That spacing matters. If both arrive at once, it feels like the party won a raffle. If spaced apart, it feels like their rescue of Renaer is rippling through different layers of Waterdeep. That is exactly what Chapter 2 is supposed to do: as word spreads, **multiple factions begin approaching them within days**.

## The practical pushback

Do not let both scenes become “join our club” speeches.

Threestrings should be a **feeler** with an invitation.
Vajra’s sending should be a **summons**.

That asymmetry is what makes the city feel large.
