The source structure is straightforward: the Harpers send the party to **The Fall of Tiamat** with a note saying **Renaer recommended them**, they are to meet **Mirt at intermission in Private Box C**, and formal attire is expected. At that meeting, **Mirt explains the Harpers, offers membership, gives the silver harp-and-moon pin to those who accept, and assigns the first Harper mission**. In the broader faction writeup, the Harpers are framed as altruists who work behind the scenes against tyranny, often conducting business in bustling taverns and using promising adventurers to uncover the truth.   

My one caution: do **not** make this a long briefing. The opera scene should feel like an initiation into a larger city, not a committee meeting with snacks.

## What Mirt is doing

Mirt is not really screening for competence. Renaer already did that.

He is screening for:

* discretion
* judgment
* whether they can handle ambiguity without becoming stupid
* whether they care about the city beyond their own immediate profit

That last point matters. The Harpers are not hiring blades. They are cultivating people who can notice what powerful people are trying to hide.

# Scene: Intermission at the Lightsinger

## Before the box

Give the opera thirty seconds of actual texture so the invitation feels real.

### Read-aloud

The Lightsinger is all gilt balconies, velvet drapery, crystal lamps, and the soft murmur of rich people pretending not to inspect one another. Servants move like clockwork. Perfume, powder, wine, and polished marble hang in the air.

Onstage, giants in lacquered masks bellow their parts in a language most of the audience clearly does not understand but pretends to appreciate deeply. The dragon queen’s fall is rendered in thunder sheets, crimson silk, and enough gold-painted scenery to ransom a prince.

At intermission, the room blooms into motion - nobles rising, fans fluttering, glasses changing hands, quiet alliances forming in the aisles.

A liveried usher appears at your elbow.

“Private Box C,” they say. “You are expected.”

That gets them out of audience mode and into intrigue mode.

---

## First look at Mirt

Play him as a man who has spent decades weaponizing the assumption that he is merely vulgar, indulgent, and old.

He should look comfortable here, but not delicate:

* rich clothes worn like armor, not fashion
* rings, good fabric, expensive cane
* seated with wine and snacks already in reach
* heavy, aging, amused
* eyes much sharper than the rest of him suggests

### Read-aloud

Private Box C smells of good brandy and old money.

A broad, aging man lounges in a chair that looks as though it has had to surrender to him by force of personality. His clothes are sumptuous without being tasteful, his rings are heavy, and his expression suggests that he has spent a lifetime collecting secrets and deciding which of them are worth the bother.

He does not rise.

Instead, he lifts his glass slightly in greeting and says:

“So. These are Renaer’s good bets.”

His gaze travels across the party once, lazily.

It is the laziest thing anyone in the room has done all evening.

And somehow the most thorough.

That is the key note: superficially indolent, actually razor-edged.

# Mirt’s pitch

## Opening exchange

He should begin with irreverence, not doctrine.

### Mirt

> “Sit, sit. If I wanted solemnity, I’d have asked you to a temple. If I wanted obedience, I’d have sent for City Watch recruits.”

He gestures at the stage outside.

> “If you have strong thoughts about the artistic merits of dragons dying in Giant, keep them brief. I am trying not to improve the production.”

Then, more pointedly:

> “Renaer says you are useful. More importantly, he says you are not fools. The city has need of both qualities, and they do not often arrive in the same bodies.”

That line does a lot of work:

* flatters the party
* invokes Renaer
* establishes Mirt as a man who values utility over heroics

---

## The actual Harper explanation

Do **not** have him recite a faction pamphlet. He should define the Harpers in practical terms.

### Mirt

> “You’ve likely met the obvious powers in Waterdeep already. Lords. Guilds. Priests. Magisters. Watch captains. Men with seals, keys, and very important chairs.”

He takes a drink.

> “The Harpers are what the city grows in the cracks between such things.”

Beat.

> “We meddle. Selectively. We pry where tyrants would prefer privacy. We notice when wealth, fear, and violence begin moving in the same direction. And when possible, we put a thumb on the scale before decent people get crushed under it.”

That tracks cleanly with the faction writeup: the Harpers work behind the scenes to keep power out of the hands of evil tyrants and are already probing the violence spreading through the city. 

Then he narrows it:

> “Sometimes that means a whispered warning. Sometimes a stolen ledger. Sometimes a rescue. Sometimes a lie told for the right reason. If you need the world sorted into bright banners and trumpet calls, you will not like us.”

That is important because Jonathan, or any overtly righteous PC, may push back on the faction’s methods.

---

## Why them?

He should make the party feel seen without overpraising them.

### Mirt

> “You helped Renaer. That earned notice. You took possession of Trollskull Manor, which means you are putting down roots. That matters more than you think. And from what I hear, you have already acquired two useful habits: being underestimated, and walking toward trouble instead of away from it.”

That lets the opera pitch connect back to the tavern. It also reinforces that Trollskull is not just a reward; it is now a node in the city.

---

## The moral hinge

This is the most important part of the scene.

Mirt should make clear that the Harpers are not asking for blind loyalty.

### Mirt

> “I am not offering you uniforms. I am not offering you permission to swagger through the streets in someone else’s righteousness. I am offering you a place among people who believe power should be watched, that cruelty should not be allowed to become fashionable, and that truth matters most precisely when it is inconvenient.”

Then:

> “We use judgment. If you cannot do that, you are dangerous. If you can do it well, you are invaluable.”

This is the line I would anchor the whole recruitment around:
**judgment, not obedience**.

It fits the Harpers much better than a standard secret-society oath.

# A boxed version of the pitch

If you want something you can nearly read verbatim:

### Mirt’s pitch

> “Let us spare one another the pageantry. The Harpers are not a club for peacocks, nor a second City Watch, nor a moral lecture dressed up as heroism. We are busy people who interfere when the wrong people acquire too much power and the right people are in danger of being buried by it.
>
> “We ask questions other folk are afraid to ask. We listen where others posture. We pass information quietly. We help the vulnerable when institutions fail them. And when some tyrant, criminal, or noble fool imagines the city belongs entirely to them, we disabuse them of the notion.
>
> “Renaer thinks you may be suited to such work. I am inclined to agree.
>
> “Mind you, this is not a request for obedience. If obedience is what I wanted, I’d recruit from barracks. What I want is judgment. Discretion. A certain ability to stand in a crowded room, hear what matters, and know which thread to pull without bringing the whole tapestry down on your own head.
>
> “If that appeals to you, I can offer acquaintance, information, and work worth doing. If it does not, finish the opera, enjoy the costumes, and we shall part with no insult taken.”

That is the cleanest version.

# How he addresses likely objections

## If Jonathan objects to secrecy

### Mirt

> “My dear boy, secrecy is not the same thing as dishonor. A hidden lantern still gives light. The question is who benefits from your honesty being visible from three streets away.”

That gives the paladin something to chew on without reducing him to “you’re naive.”

## If Aladeen leans in

### Mirt

> “A performer already understands half our trade. Audiences tell the truth constantly. They simply insist on doing it sideways.”

## If Davy stays quiet

### Mirt

> “The silent ones are often either the wisest or the most alarming. Time will sort you into your proper category.”

## If Elior asks about structure or leadership

### Mirt

> “Looser than a military order, tighter than a drinking club, and vastly more useful than either.”

## If someone asks, “What do we get out of it?”

He should not flinch at the mercenary question.

### Mirt

> “Information. Access. Aid when it is warranted. Reduced prices for certain magical necessities. Safe doors when the city grows sharp with you. And, on rare and treasured occasions, the satisfaction of having prevented something ugly before everyone else realizes it nearly happened.” 

That answer is grounded in the Harper support text without sounding like a reward table. 

---

# The offer

Once he has made the pitch, do not overcomplicate it.

### Mirt

> “So. I am offering you association with the Harpers of Waterdeep. Not all of you must say yes. Not all of you should, unless the answer is honestly yours.”

Then he produces the pins.

### Read-aloud

From within his coat, Mirt draws a small velvet case. Nestled inside are several silver pins, each fashioned in the shape of a harp cradled within a crescent moon.

He opens the case on the low table between you.

“Those who accept,” he says, “take one.”

That matches the published faction scene directly. 

A useful flourish:
he does **not** hand them out himself. He makes them choose.

---

# The first mission

Once at least one PC accepts, Mirt moves immediately to business. That is correct to the source: acceptance and first mission happen in the same meeting. The level-2 Harper mission is to locate **Maxeene**, a talking dray mare, and learn whether she overheard the identities or whereabouts of Zhent operatives. 

Do not dump the whole mission text like a quest card. Let Mirt brief it.

### Mirt

> “Good. Then let us begin with something modest.
>
> “One of the drays working the city is pulled by a mare named Maxeene. Unusual beast. Clever. Observant. Talks, which in my experience is rarely an improvement, but there we are.
>
> “I have reason to believe she recently overheard useful talk from Zhentarim operatives. Find her. Convince her you are speaking on our behalf. Learn what she knows. More importantly, learn whether you know how to ask the right questions of the right witness.”

If you want to preserve the mechanics in your own notes:

* Investigation to locate her
* Persuasion to get her to trust them
* she recalls a sun elf and half-orc bodyguard dropped at the Yawning Portal, matching Davil and Yagra 

But Mirt should not present that as an exercise in dice. For him, this is a test of fieldcraft:
can they find an odd witness, gain trust, and extract usable intel?

---

# Strong ending line

End with something sharper than “welcome aboard.”

My preferred closer:

### Mirt

> “Waterdeep is full of people who believe the city belongs to them because they are rich enough, cruel enough, or old enough. They are mistaken.
>
> “If you care to help prove them mistaken, wear the pin only when it serves you, keep your ears open, and do not become idiots merely because someone flatter than me calls you heroes.”

Then he raises his glass toward the stage as the bell rings for the second act.

> “Now. Back to the dragon. She dies magnificently in the third movement.”

That gets you out cleanly.

---

# Why this version works

It preserves the printed function of the scene:

* opera invitation
* intermission meeting
* Mirt offers Harper membership
* pins for those who accept
* first mission immediately follows 

But it also gives Mirt a real thesis:
**the Harpers are about judgment, quiet intervention, and keeping power from curdling into tyranny**. That is much closer to the faction’s actual identity than a generic “join our secret society” speech. 

The mistake to avoid is making Mirt too avuncular or too silly. He can be bawdy, indulgent, and theatrical, but the party should leave that box understanding one thing clearly:

**this old man noticed them because he is dangerous in exactly the way the Harpers approve of.**
