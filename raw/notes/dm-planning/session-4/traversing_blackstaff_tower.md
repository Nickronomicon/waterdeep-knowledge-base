# BLACKSTAFF TOWER ARRIVAL/TRAVERSAL

## [return to prep sheet](./prep_sheet.md#traversing-blackstaff-towner)

Good correction. If Blackstaff Tower feels like a normal wizard office building, it undersells Vajra and the institution immediately.

You want the tower traversal to feel:

* disorienting
* arcane
* curated rather than hostile
* impossible to map afterward
* effortless for invited guests

So the experience should not be a puzzle, trap sequence, or skill challenge. It should be a **guided impossibility**.

## How to frame it

The party is not “finding their way” through the tower. The tower is **delivering them**.

Doors open before they knock. Stairs arrive where needed. Rooms imply continuity they do not possess. Distances compress and expand according to a logic no one can quite hold in memory. By the time they reach Vajra, each PC should be able to describe the route vividly, and none of those descriptions should agree.

That gives you the fever-dream effect without creating friction.

---

# DM-facing Blackstaff Tower Arrival Sequence

## Core principle

Every transition should do one of these:

* contradict mundane space
* respond subtly to the PCs
* suggest purpose without explanation
* remain calm enough that nobody thinks they are under attack

The tower is weird, but it is not menacing to invited guests.

---

## Boxed read-aloud: Exterior approach

> Blackstaff Tower rises dark against the deepening sky, narrow in one glance and impossibly broad in the next. The entire edifice appeared to be made of smooth black stone, with a few stairs leading up to a blank stone face.
>
> The Tower wall remains blank until you reach the foot of the steps. Then, without sound or visible hand, a seam forms in the rock and the wall opens inward.

---

## Boxed read-aloud: First threshold

> The air changes as soon as you cross. Outside was evening. Inside is lamplit stillness, cool stone, the smell of paper, rain, cedar smoke, and something sharper underneath—ozone, perhaps, or a spell half-remembered.
>
> The entry hall should fit inside the tower you just saw. It does not. The ceiling disappears into shadow above a cagework of balconies, bridges, hanging lamps, and slow-turning brass forms that may be astrolabes or may be something much stranger.

---

# The traversal structure

Run this as **3 to 5 surreal beats**, each brief. No rolls unless a player does something very specific. An invisible servant, silent apprentice, animated lantern, or simply the architecture itself keeps them moving.

A good pattern is:

1. impossible first impression
2. one personalized uncanny moment
3. one spatial contradiction
4. one quiet, beautiful image
5. arrival in Vajra’s presence

---

# Surreal beat options

Use 3–5 of these. Do not use all of them.

## 1. The staircase that does not agree

> A stair curls up along the wall ahead, but when you begin climbing it, the sensation is not ascent. Your ears pop as though you were descending, and when you glance over the rail the front door is somehow above you, far away, hanging in the air like something remembered incorrectly.

### DM note

No danger. No save. Just wrongness.

---

## 2. The window that shows the wrong place

> You pass a narrow window. Beyond it is not Castle Ward at dusk, but bright noon over a rain-lashed sea. A heartbeat later the view has changed again: snow falling sideways over rooftops you do not recognize. No one else in the hall reacts.

### DM note

If a PC stops to stare, the next passerby or floating light simply continues onward, making it clear this is normal here.

---

## 3. The hallway that edits itself

> A corridor stretches ahead lined with doors of black wood and hammered brass. You look away for only a moment—perhaps to answer a companion—and when you look back, the doors are all red, the brass is silver, and there are fewer of them than before.

### DM note

Let one player notice the change clearly. Another player noticed a different change. Good.

---

## 4. The room passed through too quickly

> A doorway opens onto a vast circular chamber crowded with floating candles, suspended books, and a model of the city built from translucent blue light. Before you can take more than a breath of it, the route bends, the door is behind you, and you are somewhere else.

### DM note

This sells scale. Do not linger long enough for exploration.

---

## 5. The impossible crossing

> You are led across a narrow bridge of dark stone hung in open air. Beneath it lies not a floor, but a depth filled with dim constellations, drifting motes, and the vague suggestion of lower chambers turning slowly in darkness. Halfway across, you become aware that the bridge is much wider than it first appeared, wide enough for a cart. By the time you reach the far side, it is narrow again.

---

## 6. Whispering portraits or statues

> A row of portraits watches from the wall: Blackstaffs past, severe and patient. One seems to track Jonathan with frank appraisal. Another inclines its head almost imperceptibly toward Elior. A third looks at Davy and frowns, not in suspicion but in concentration, as though trying to read smudged ink.

### DM note

This is excellent for personal unease. Not accusatory. Just seen.

---

## 7. The library breath

> You pass an open arch and hear the soft, collective sound of thousands of pages turning somewhere in the dark, as though the tower had inhaled through a library.

### DM note

Short, elegant, memorable.

---

## 8. The lantern guide

A floating lantern of blue-white witchlight drifts ahead of them. Whenever they slow, it waits. Whenever they begin speaking too loudly, it dims in reproach. When they resume moving, it brightens.

### DM note

This is your navigation solution. The tower is bizarre, but the path is effortless because they are being guided.

---

## 9. The floor that remembers weather

> The floor beneath your boots changes from polished stone to old wood to cool tile and back again over the course of half a dozen strides. On one stretch, someone’s footprints appear briefly in rainwater ahead of you and evaporate before they can be examined.

---

## 10. The same apprentice, impossibly repeated

> A young apprentice in gray robes steps aside to let you pass, arms full of scroll tubes. Two turns later, the same apprentice—same face, same burden, same mildly frazzled expression—is already ahead of you doing exactly the same thing. If this troubles them, they have learned not to show it.

### DM note

Good subtle fever-dream detail.

# Making it feel personal without becoming about the PCs

A good fever dream is never random; it feels almost meaningful without becoming explicit. Give each character one small resonance.

## Examples for your party

* **Jonathan**: a polished suit of ceremonial armor turns its helm toward him as he passes, then stills.
* **Davy**: a mirror reflects him half a step late, or for a heartbeat shows the “wrong” silhouette before correcting.
* **Elior**: his scar warms as he passes a sealed black door etched in silver script.
* **Broethuul**: a hanging garden arch briefly smells like autumn leaves and cold gold sunlight.

None of this should be explained. The tower notices things. That is enough.

---

# Navigation without difficulty

You specifically do **not** want the weirdness to become logistical friction. So make these things consistently true:

## The route always advances

There is always an obvious next step:

* open door
* waiting lantern
* silent attendant
* staircase already aligned
* curtain drawing aside

## Nobody asks for directions

The tower never leaves them at a junction with uncertainty.

## Attempts to backtrack are politely defeated

If someone glances behind them:

* the hall looks different
* the door is gone
* the stair is now a wall niche
* the route back is present but emotionally unavailable, like waking from a dream and knowing you cannot return to it

Not a trap. Just not offered.

---

# Arrival at Vajra

After the surreal approach, Vajra’s chamber should be calmer, simpler, and more controlled. That contrast is important. She is not just another weird detail in the tower. She is the person in the building most at home in it.

## Boxed read-aloud: Arrival chamber

> At last the tower’s strangeness narrows instead of expanding. One final door opens onto a chamber that feels almost severe after everything before it: high-ceilinged, spare, ordered. Lamps burn with steady light. Shelves stand where shelves ought to stand. A writing table occupies the center of the room with the confidence of something that has always known exactly where it belongs.
>
> Vajra Safahr stands beside it, one hand resting lightly on the back of a chair as though she has been expecting you not merely tonight, but at precisely this moment and by precisely this path.

This makes Vajra feel like the fixed point inside the dream.

---

# A good closing line from Vajra

> “Most people remember their first walk through the tower incorrectly,” she says. “That is usually a sign it went well.”

That line does three useful things:

* confirms the experience was real
* normalizes the weirdness
* reassures the party they were not being tested or toyed with maliciously

# Short version you can run at the table

If you want the compressed version:

1. Door opens on its own
2. Entry hall is far too large for the tower
3. Floating lantern guides them
4. Stair feels like descent while going up
5. Window shows the wrong sky
6. Hall changes when no one looks directly at it
7. Portraits seem to notice specific PCs
8. Final chamber is suddenly calm and rational
9. Vajra says, “Most people remember their first walk through the tower incorrectly.”

That is enough to make the place unforgettable.

---

# One caution

Do not stack the weirdness so hard that players start trying to solve it. The moment it feels like a puzzle, the mood changes.

Use sensory certainty and causal uncertainty:

* they know what they saw
* they do not know how it was possible
* they are never asked to resolve the contradiction

That keeps it dreamlike instead of gamified.
