# The Crommor Incident
*A Brief Public Recollection (DR 1489)*

In the Year of the Warrior Princess (1489 DR), during the grand ceremony celebrating the ascension of Laeral Silverhand as Open Lord of Waterdeep, a moment of high ceremony turned unexpectedly infamous.

The noble House Crommor had been granted the honor of presenting a ceremonial bugle to the new Open Lord, a finely wrought instrument meant to symbolize vigilance and renewal under her leadership. The task of carrying the piece forward fell to young Anastasia Crommor, daughter of the house.

Before she reached the dais, she stumbled.

The bugle fell from her grasp and struck the cobblestones, breaking beyond repair. The sharp crack of metal echoed through the square, silencing much of the gathered crowd.

From the ground, Anastasia loudly accused another noble present (Lord Victoro Cassalanter) of having tripped her. Cassalanter denied the charge immediately and publicly, suggesting instead that nerves and inexperience had gotten the better of the young woman.

What followed was not violence, but something perhaps more enduring: spectacle.

Members of House Crommor attempted to remove Anastasia from the square, but she repeated her accusation several times, audibly enough that much of the assembly heard it clearly. The exchange became the subject of immediate and intense gossip throughout the city.

No formal charges were ever brought.

No inquiry was officially recorded.

Yet in the weeks that followed, society divided into quiet camps: those who believed Anastasia had spoken truth in a moment of shock, and those who believed she had embarrassed herself and her house before the Open Lord.

Whether coincidence or consequence, House Crommor’s influence appeared to wane in the months afterward. Invitations lessened. Business alliances cooled. Rumors flourished.

The incident passed into Waterdhavian culture with remarkable speed.

A street performer by the name of Aladeen popularized a comedic routine known as “The Crommor Collapse,” in which he would theatrically fall near a passerby and shout, “You tripped me!” The act spread rapidly, eventually appearing in taverns, festivals, and even lighthearted noble entertainments.

Within a year, the phrase itself had entered common speech, shorthand for an accusation delivered too loudly, too publicly, or without proof.

Today, most citizens remember the Crommor Incident less as a political scandal and more as a lesson in the peril of public embarrassment.

Whether it was clumsiness, conspiracy, or simple misfortune remains (officially) unresolved.
