# Aladeen of the Laughing Lyre
## The Night of the Crommor Collapse
Aladeen swears by every stage he has ever performed on:

He never meant it to become cruel.

He was there that day.

Not among the nobles. Not within the court. But close enough to see the balcony. Close enough to hear the crack of stone when the bugle struck the cobbles below.

Gods.

He still remembers that sound.

It wasn’t dramatic. It wasn’t grand. It was small and wrong, like a joke told at the wrong funeral.

Then came the shout.

*“You tripped me!”*

Clear. Sharp. Desperate.

And people laughed.

Not because it was funny.

Because no one knew what else to do.

The laugh came first. He remembers that clearly. That strange, reflexive sound a crowd makes when reality fractures and no one is ready to acknowledge it.

Aladeen watched the faces. Shock curdling into gossip. Concern mutating into performance. The city deciding, in real time, that this would be a story rather than a tragedy.

He does not know if Victoro Cassalanter tripped her. Truly, he doesn’t.

He remembers Cassalanter looking startled. Not angry. Not smug. Just sharp-eyed. Like a man who noticed something go wrong one heartbeat too late.

And then the moment passed.

Waterdeep is very good at letting moments pass.

---

## The Night It Became a Performance
Days later, at a tavern show with The Laughing Lyre, the crowd was sour. The energy sagged. The performance was sliding toward failure.

Aladeen felt that familiar edge, the point where tension curdles into hostility.

And without thinking, he staggered backward mid-line. His heel caught empty air. His arms flailed in perfect theatrical panic.

He fell.

Perfectly.

And from the floor, in an imitation so precise it startled even him, he cried:

*“You tripped me!”*

The tavern erupted.

Not the awkward laugh from the courtyard.

This was real laughter. Released tension. A city exhaling.

Someone shouted, “Do it again!” Another collapsed in imitation. Tankards pounded tables.

In that moment, Aladeen told himself what he always tells himself:

He was making it smaller.

Making it survivable.

Bleeding off the tension so it wouldn’t turn into something uglier.

The bit gained a name by morning:

**The Crommor Collapse.**

It spread. Actors copied it. Street performers exaggerated it. The fall became broader, the voice shriller, the indignation more absurd. The city reshaped a young noblewoman’s humiliation into slapstick.

And Aladeen let it happen.

Because it worked.

Because it was brilliant.

Because he was good at this.

---

## What He Tells Himself
He never met Anastasia Crommor. Not properly.

Just a girl on the stones, shouting the truth (or what she believed was the truth) while the city chose a joke instead.

He tells himself he didn’t start the laughter.

He tells himself he merely redirected it.

He tells himself satire holds power accountable.

He tells himself he made something dangerous into something harmless.

He tells himself she must have landed on her feet somewhere far from Waterdeep.

He tells himself that because the other possibility, that they all helped bury her under applause, is harder to live with.

---

## The Aftermath
For a brief, blazing stretch of time, Aladeen was famous.

Recognized in markets. Welcomed into taverns. Fed and housed “by popular demand.” People begged him to do the fall. To shout the line. To make them laugh.

Then came the quiet signals.

A servant who watched too closely. A noble patron who did not smile. A scrap of parchment left in his open lute case:

> “Mind your footing.”

No signature.

None needed.

House Crommor did not respond publicly.

But nobles remember.

---

## The Truth Beneath the Smile
Aladeen believes in creativity. In bold action. In making the world better than it was when he found it.

He believes no one should be above laughter.

He also knows laughter is not neutral.

Sometimes it protects.

Sometimes it wounds.

He cannot help himself when a moment begs for performance. His tongue is sharp. His instincts are sharper.

If he had that night again, would he still fall?

He does not know.

He suspects he would.

And that may be the part that troubles him most.
