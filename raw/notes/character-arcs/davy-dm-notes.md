# Davy
**Player:** Hanna

**Secret:** Davy, the young street tough, is actually Anastasia Crommor, a noble girl from House Crommor posing as a boy.

## Anastasia Crommor

During the ceremony honoring Laeral Silverhand’s ascension to Open Lord of Waterdeep (DR 1489), Anastasia was given the honor of presenting a beautifully crafted bugle to the new lord. While carrying it forward, in full view of the city she’s jostled (intentionally?) by Victoro Cassalanter. The bugle falls to the cobblestones, where it is damaged beyond restoration. Anastasia, still on the ground, exclaims loudly, “You tripped me!” for the whole court and city to hear. Cassalanter loudly denies it and mocks her for her clumsiness. The Crommor family, humiliated, begins pulling Anastasia aside in an attempt to save face. Anastasia instead keeps loudly insisting, “He tripped me!”

This scandal becomes the talk of the town for the next few weeks. The Crommors are humiliated and Davin Crommor (the head of the family) can’t show his face in public. Whether due to political fallout from this incident or mere circumstance, the Crommor’s standing in the city takes a hit. This drop in fortune doesn’t cause the furor to die down, it only enflames the gossip, and the Crommor’s spend the better part of a year as “tabloid darlings.” One aspiring street performer, Aladeen, invents the “Crommor Collapse,” where he would brush up near someone before falling dramatically to the ground, screaming, “You tripped me!” in the same fashion as Anastasia. This performance quickly caught on and inspired imitators, eventually making its way into even high society plays in a winking fashion. Davin Crommor was overheard saying he would “have that damned bard strangled,” which led to another round in the tabloids.

Under this pressure, Davin got into an argument with Anastasia, proclaiming that he would feed and house her as long as the law required, but not a moment longer. In anger, Anastasia fled the villa and spent the next year (DR 1490) living rough on the streets of Waterdeep. Learning to thieve by trial and error, Anastasia had a few run-ins with the law. She cut her hair and passed as a young boy, telling the city watch that her name was “Davin” or “Davy” each time she was arrested

Shortly after one of her longer periods of incarceration, Anastasia (now Davy) met and befriended a newly-hired barmaid named Bonnie at the Yawning Portal. Bonnie took pity on Davy and offered him food from the kitchen, even at the risk to her own employment. Shortly thereafter, an undertaker named Dorgar with a vaguely menacing aura but truly kind heart found Davy sleeping amongst the crypts in the CIty of the Dead Ward. Dorgar took pity on Davy and offered him a place to stay in his domicile on Blackthorn Alley, near The Plinth. Davy takes him up on his offer, as it beats sleeping on the cold mausoleum steps. Eventually overcoming the old undertaker’s cadaverous appearance and menacing air, Davy confides in Dorgar and tells him the entire story. In turn, Dorgar reveals that he’s a cleric of Myrkur, and it’s his responsibility to collect the dead and deliver them to Kelemvor for judgement. Dorgar offers Davy a role as his apprentice, learning to shepherd the newly deceased to the afterlife. Davy accepts and spends a night and a day fasting in the throne chamber of a temple to Myrkur deep beneath the City of the Dead.

Continuing to visit Bonnie at the Yawning Portal, but now as a paying (albeit poor) customer, Davy meets Bonnie’s friend Mattrim “Threestrings” Mereg. Threestrings is an enthusiastic young bard and quite good looking, which is good because he’s painfully awkward and not very talented. Threestrings is at the Portal most nights along with his friend and fellow bard, none other than Aladeen, the street performer that kicked off so much chaos so long ago. Aladeen, despite earning fame by mimicking Anastasia, does not recognize Davy. Davy sees this as a perfect opportunity to confirm his cover and continues to associate with the bards, even going so far as to join in when they playfully vie for Bonnie’s affections.

Davy now spends his mornings in study with Dorgar, his days near the market to keep his thieving skills sharp, and his evenings carousing at the Yawning Portal. It’s on one such evening, after the ground floor of the tavern finished hearing a rousing tale from the famed sage Volothamp Geddarn, that our story begins.

## Chat GPT version:

Anastasia Crommor had never expected to be remembered by all of Waterdeep.
On the day of the grand ceremony celebrating Laeral Silverhand’s ascension to Open Lord in 1489 DR, she was meant to be little more than a footnote, another well-dressed noble daughter performing a symbolic honor. The Crommor family had been chosen to present a ceremonial bugle to the new Open Lord: a masterfully crafted piece, polished to a mirror shine, meant to herald a new era for the city.
Anastasia carried it forward through the crowded court, heart pounding, aware of thousands of eyes on her. She never reached Laeral.
As she stepped across the stones, someone struck her shoulder hard enough to send her sprawling. The bugle flew from her hands and shattered against the cobblestones with a sound far louder than any note it might ever have played. As Anastasia lay stunned on the ground, shock turned instantly to fury.
“You tripped me!” she cried, loud enough for the court (and half the city beyond it) to hear.
The man standing nearest her was Victoro Cassalanter.
Cassalanter laughed, denied everything, and mockingly suggested that the young Crommor girl had simply proven herself clumsy under pressure. Courtiers murmured. The Crommor family rushed forward, trying to haul Anastasia away, desperate to salvage dignity from the wreckage.
Anastasia refused to be silenced. Again and again she shouted, “He tripped me!” until the words echoed in the square.
For weeks afterward, Waterdeep had nothing else to talk about.
Whether by politics, coincidence, or quiet manipulation, the Crommor family’s influence began to wane. Invitations dried up. Business partners grew distant. The scandal refused to fade, feeding instead on the family’s very public discomfort. By the end of the year, the Crommors had become the city’s favorite cautionary tale: highborn humiliation served daily, with embellishments.
A young street performer named Aladeen turned the affair into farce. His act, dubbed The Crommor Collapse, involved brushing past a bystander, throwing himself to the ground, and screaming “You tripped me!” to roaring laughter. The routine spread like wildfire. Soon imitators appeared, and before long even refined stage plays in noble salons included winking references to the infamous fall.
When Davin Crommor, head of the family, was overheard vowing to “have that damned bard strangled,” it only earned the tabloids another round of ink.
The pressure at home became unbearable. Davin’s temper finally broke in a bitter argument with his daughter. He told Anastasia he would provide food and shelter only as long as the law demanded it, and not a moment more.
She left the villa that night.
The year 1490 DR found Anastasia Crommor living on the streets of Waterdeep. She learned to steal because hunger demanded it, and learned quickly. She was caught often enough, but clever enough to adapt. She cut her hair short, dirtied her face, and began passing as a boy. When questioned by the City Watch, she gave her name as “Davin.” Sometimes “Davy.”
It was as Davy that she met Bonnie.
Freshly hired at the Yawning Portal, Bonnie noticed the half-starved boy who lingered near the door and took pity on him. She slipped him food from the kitchen when she could, even when it risked her position. After one particularly long stint in a Watch cell, Davy found himself sleeping among the tombs of the City of the Dead, only to be discovered by an undertaker named Dorgar.
Dorgar looked like something out of a nightmare: gaunt, pale, and possessed of a voice that sounded like it belonged underground. Yet his heart was gentle. He offered Davy a place to sleep in his home off Blackthorn Alley, near the Plinth. Cold stone steps were traded for a roof, and suspicion eventually gave way to trust.
In time, Davy told Dorgar everything.
Dorgar, in turn, revealed his own truth: he was a cleric of Myrkul, tasked with gathering the dead and delivering their souls onward to Kelemvor’s judgment. He offered Davy an apprenticeship, to learn the quiet work of shepherding the departed. Davy accepted, and spent a night and a day fasting alone in the throne chamber of a hidden Myrkulite temple beneath the City of the Dead.
Life settled into a strange rhythm.
Mornings were spent studying with Dorgar. Days were passed near the markets, keeping old habits sharp. Evenings belonged to the Yawning Portal, where Davy now paid for his drinks (barely) and where Bonnie had introduced him to her friend, Mattrim “Threestrings” Mereg.
Threestrings was handsome, enthusiastic, painfully awkward, and only marginally talented. He was almost always accompanied by his fellow bard and closest friend: Aladeen, the very performer who had once built his fame on Anastasia Crommor’s humiliation.
Aladeen never recognized Davy.
For Anastasia, now well practiced in being someone else, that anonymity felt like proof her disguise was complete. She joined the bards’ banter, their songs, and even their playful competition for Bonnie’s attention, all while standing shoulder-to-shoulder with the living echo of her past.
And so she lived: thief, apprentice, drinking companion, ghost of a noble daughter.
On one such evening, after the taproom had finished applauding a tale spun by the famed sage Volothamp Geddarm himself, fate finally stirred.
That is where the story truly begins.

**“She was drunk.”**

**“The bugle was cursed.”**
> Someone heard it was meant to sound during the ceremony and didn’t. The gods were offended. The fall was inevitable.



#### Noble & Salon Gossip (Upper Class)
**“The Crommors staged it.”**
> A desperate ploy to gain sympathy and attention during Laeral’s ascension. It backfired spectacularly.


“Cassalanter was sending a message.”
 Not to Anastasia—to her father. A reminder of where real power in Waterdeep sits.


“Laeral noticed everything.”
 The Open Lord didn’t intervene publicly, but she remembers the look on Anastasia’s face. Some say that matters more than the truth.


“It wasn’t about the fall at all.”
 The Crommors were already slipping. The incident merely gave everyone permission to stop pretending otherwise.

Scene One: The Open Lord Asks a Simple Question
(Laeral probing Davy’s past)
Setup
 This works best after the party has done something competent but not world-shaking—enough to warrant notice, not gratitude. Laeral requests a brief audience. No guards inside the room. Tea is served.
Tone
 Calm. Observant. Kind in a way that feels more dangerous than anger.

Laeral Silverhand studies Davy the way a scholar studies a familiar text—less interested in the words than in the margins.
She does not ask his name at first.
Instead, she gestures to the window overlooking Waterdeep and says, conversationally,
 “Do you know how many people have fallen in this city?”
She waits just long enough for the question to feel rhetorical before continuing.
“From towers. From favor. From grace.”
 A faint smile. “Sometimes all at once.”
Her eyes flick, briefly, to Davy’s hands.
“You walk like someone who learned the streets quickly. That usually means you arrived there suddenly.”
She pours tea. Steam curls between them.
“Tell me,” she says gently, “did Waterdeep cast you out… or did you leave before it could?”
If pressed, she never says Crommor.
 She never mentions the ceremony.
 She never says Cassalanter.
But if Davy deflects or lies, she nods as though that answer confirms something.
At the end of the meeting, Laeral says only this:
“There was a warning meant for me, once. It never reached my hands.”
 Her gaze settles fully on Davy now.
 “When people try to speak truth in public, they often pay for it. I try to remember who those people were.”
She dismisses the party warmly.
Only as Davy turns to leave does Laeral add, almost as an afterthought:
“If you ever remember something you think I should have known sooner… my door is open.”
GM Notes
Laeral knows the warning existed. She does not know who carried it.


If Davy reveals anything close to the truth, Laeral does not react immediately.


This scene seeds trust without granting protection—yet.

Scene Two: Aladeen Learns What the Joke Cost
(Aladeen discovers the truth)
Setup
 Late night. After a performance. Maybe after Aladeen bombs a set or watches Davy do something quietly brave. This should not be public.
Tone
 Regret. Realization. No absolution handed out.

Aladeen finds the truth the worst possible way:
 accidentally.
It starts with a name—Crommor—spoken by someone who shouldn’t know it. Maybe a drunken noble. Maybe an old pamphlet. Maybe a priest who knew Dorgar.
The pieces don’t click all at once. They grind.
When it finally lands, Aladeen goes pale.
He waits until they’re alone.
“You never told me,” he says, voice thin, “your family name.”
If Davy says nothing, Aladeen keeps going.
“I was there,” he says. “I saw it happen. Gods, I built half my reputation on it.”
 A bitter laugh. “I told myself it was harmless. That it was about the city, not the girl.”
He looks at Davy—really looks.
“You were telling the truth,” he says quietly.
 “And I taught Waterdeep to laugh instead.”
There’s no big apology speech. He doesn’t beg forgiveness.
Instead, he offers this:
“I still know how to make a crowd listen.”
 A pause.
 “If you ever want the city to hear something else… I’ll help. No jokes.”
Whether Davy forgives him or not is left entirely to the player.
GM Notes
Aladeen is shaken but not broken—this becomes a choice point for him.


He may later weaponize his fame against Cassalanter… or against himself.


This truth can strain or strengthen the party dynamic in a very organic way.




The Crommor Warning
(Unsigned draft, recovered copy; the original was sealed inside the ceremonial bugle)
To the one who now bears the burden of Waterdeep,
I beg you to read this not as accusation, but as alarm.
There are houses in this city whose prosperity has accelerated beyond the patience of chance. Their accounts balance too cleanly. Their failures vanish too quickly. I have traced this pattern across contracts, loans, and favors rendered where none were requested.
One family in particular has prospered in the shadow of your ascension. Their fortunes rose before the public knew they would need to rise.
I believe—though I cannot prove—that this prosperity is not mortal in origin.
I have reason to fear that a bargain was struck during the months surrounding your elevation, guided by whispers that promised salvation for the city but demanded payment in ways not yet visible. If so, then Waterdeep has already been pledged, not in whole, but in pieces small enough to ignore.
I do not know the full terms. I know only this: infernal contracts do not reward virtue, and they never forgive delay.
If you receive this warning too late, then I pray I am wrong.
If you receive it in time, then I urge you to watch those who smile most confidently when speaking of civic duty.
I have taken steps to ensure this message reaches you without passing through the usual hands. Should it fail, I ask forgiveness of the city.
—A loyal servant of Waterdeep
